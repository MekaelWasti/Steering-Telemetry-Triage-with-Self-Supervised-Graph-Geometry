Architected measurable analyst-workflow metrics to validate clustering quality
You're treating "analyst has an easier time" as if it were an unmeasurable vibe, and it isn't — it's a workflow, and any workflow you can describe, you can simulate. That's the whole trick. The reason a metric we design would have everything to do with the analyst is that we design it by writing down what the analyst actually does in front of your cluster map, step by step, and then scoring each step with the labels we already have. The metric isn't a substitute for the analyst experience; it's the analyst experience, formalized and made repeatable.

Walk through the workflow you just described. The analyst opens the map. Their eyes go to clusters, they read labels, they drill into a few that look interesting, they sample some sessions, they dismiss or escalate, they move on. "Not wasting time on 99% garbage" means: the number of sessions they must inspect before they've found the malicious activity is small. That sentence is already a metric. Formally:

Effort-to-recall. Simulate a triage policy: rank the clusters in some order (more on ordering below), have the simulated analyst "inspect" each cluster by sampling its exemplars, and count how many sessions they touch before reaching, say, 90% of the bad-user-labeled sessions. One number, in units of analyst effort. Your "99% garbage" goal translates directly: a good map gets to 90% recall having inspected 1–2% of the data; a bad one makes you wade through 40%. When you compare TF-IDF against TF-IDF+cmdline tokens, this number tells you which map the analyst would rather live in. Nothing academic about it.

Contamination, which is the metric your bulk-dismiss workflow secretly depends on. The entire economic value of clustering for triage is that an analyst can sample three sessions from a cluster labeled "scheduled backup activity," see it's boring, and dismiss the other 8,000 in one gesture. That gesture is only safe if clusters are pure. So measure the failure mode directly: what fraction of malicious sessions live inside clusters that are overwhelmingly benign? Every such session is one the analyst will confidently dismiss — the worst outcome, worse than no clustering, because the map actively laundered the attack. A representation that scores well on pretty-cluster aesthetics but smears Caldera sessions one-each into giant benign clusters is a bad map, and this metric catches it while your eyes can't.

Map readability itself. The 2D plot is a projection, and "discernible on the plot" has a measurable shadow too: do malicious sessions' nearest neighbors in the 2D layout share their label, and do they form spatially compact blobs rather than salt-and-pepper scatter? If malice is compact in the projection, an analyst can literally see it; if the kNN label agreement collapses going from high-dim to 2D, your embedding might be fine and your projection is the problem — which is exactly the kind of localization you can't do by staring at the plot.

Your real-time sorting idea is also a metric in disguise. "Sort clusters by filters" is a ranking problem: rank clusters by some suspiciousness signal (rarity, LOLBAS density, label content, size), and score it as precision@k — do malicious-containing clusters surface in the top of the sort? This cleanly separates two questions your current framing fuses together: whether the embedding put attacks into findable clusters (the ceiling), and whether the UI ranking surfaces those clusters first (how close you get to the ceiling). Both measurable, independently.

And one for the Toponymy layer specifically: a blinded label-informativeness check. Show an LLM judge (or a teammate) only the cluster's generated label and keyphrases — no data — and ask "would you escalate this?" If clusters containing bad-user activity don't get escalated from their labels alone, the labels aren't doing their triage job regardless of how coherent they read.

Now, the honest caveat, because rigor demands it: the ground-truth version of your goal is a human study — real analysts, time-to-detection, miss rates, with the map versus without. That's the outer loop, it's expensive, and you run it rarely (and eventually you should, once, because it's also the result that makes the project undeniable to your team and any reviewer). The proxy metrics above are the inner loop: they run in seconds, they're built from the analyst's own decision procedure plus labels ACME4 already gives you, and they correlate with the human outcome by construction — because each one is a step of the human workflow with a number attached. That's the 80/20 split here: simulate the analyst daily, consult the real one quarterly.

The practical upshot hasn't changed from my earlier verdict, but your pushback sharpens it: don't go pick some clustering metric off a shelf (silhouette score, Davies-Bouldin — these genuinely would deserve your skepticism, since they measure geometric tidiness, not analyst utility). Instead spend one day writing the simulated-analyst harness: rank clusters → sample exemplars → count effort → score recall and contamination against the bad-user labels in the gold split. Maybe 150 lines of code over process_uber_summary. From that day forward, "is this map better for the analyst" stops being a debate at your group meetings and becomes a number on a curve — which, given your frustration with the group's non-convergence, might be the most valuable thing the metric does.

Good — that's the right division of labor: the metric is the instrument, the embedding is the work. Here's the actual build plan for "take sessions, order them, link them, embed them in a way that's worthwhile." I'll give it as a dependency chain, because the ordering matters: each stage produces something usable even if you stop there, and each one feeds the next.

Step 1 — Build the trajectory object (this is the real foundation, ~2-3 days)
Right now your unit is a process row with a bag of 25 features. Everything you want — ordering, linking, sequence embedding, graph escalation later — depends on first materializing a different data structure: the linked, time-ordered session. Concretely, over process_uber_summary (DuckDB straight on the parquet is the comfortable tool at 500k rows):

Reconstruct process trees from pid_hash → parent linkage. Each tree root (or each tree pruned at a depth/fan-out cap) becomes one session document. Keep your per-process sessions too — you'll want the comparison.
Within each tree, order events by timestamp into a single sequence.
Tokenize each event into a discrete vocabulary item. This is the make-or-break design decision, and the practical recipe is a compound token: event_type + coarse object category (file-in-userdir, registry-run-key, network-external...) + process identity, plus separate tokens for cmdline n-grams and the ancestor path (winword>cmd>powershell). Aim for a vocab of a few thousand tokens — fine enough to be discriminative, coarse enough that patterns repeat across hosts.
What you've built at the end of this step is a corpus of "sentences" where each session is a token sequence with causal linkage baked into the tokens. Every embedding method for the rest of the project consumes this one artifact. This, not any model, is where most of the signal gets won or lost — it's the part worth a day of staring at actual Caldera sessions to sanity-check that the tokens visibly tell the story.

Step 2 — Embedding v1: order-aware but trainless (~2 days)
Before any neural model: TF-IDF over unigrams + bigrams + trigrams of your token sequences, then truncated SVD down to ~300 dims. This is your current pipeline's direct descendant — same math family, but now the features carry order (bigrams) and causality (path tokens). It drops into Toponymy unchanged. Score it on the harness. Two reasons not to skip this even though it feels unglamorous: it's plausibly 70% of the achievable gain, and it's the baseline that tells you whether the neural model in step 3 is earning its complexity.

Step 3 — Embedding v2: self-supervised sequence encoder (~1-2 weeks)
This is the "worthwhile embedding" you're actually after. Small transformer encoder — 4 layers, ~10-20M params, trains on a single GPU in hours at your corpus size — over the token sequences from step 1, trained with a label-free objective. Two candidates, and I'd run the second:

LogBERT-style masked event modeling (mask 15% of tokens, predict them) — learns co-occurrence and local order.
Next-window prediction — encode events in window w, predict the token distribution of window w+1. This is the portable objective from the pruning paper discussion, and it's better matched to your kill-chain target because it forces the representation to encode what this behavior leads to, not just what it contains. A recon stage and a cred-access stage may share many tokens; they differ in what follows them.
Session embedding = mean-pool of the encoder states (or a CLS token). Feed to Toponymy, score on the harness, compare against v1. The decision rule you wanted to avoid paralysis: if v2 beats v1 meaningfully on effort-to-recall/contamination, the sequence thesis is confirmed and you iterate there; if it doesn't, that's your evidence that order-signal saturates early and the residual gap is causal-structure-shaped — which is precisely the justification (or not) for the GNN escalation, now made by data instead of debate. Either outcome converges.

One training detail that matters for your use case: hold out the malicious hosts/days from pretraining entirely. You want the embedding to organize behavior, learned from benign mass, and you want zero suspicion that it memorized the attacks it's later evaluated on.

Step 4 — The live map and real-time reclustering (~3-4 days, parallelizable)
Here's the part where the architecture choice makes "recluster in real time" nearly free instead of a systems project. The expensive operations are embedding (GPU, but precomputed once per representation version) and the cluster-tree construction. Three observations:

Toponymy already gives you multi-resolution for free. Its condensation procedure produces layered fine-to-coarse clusterings where every point exists at every layer. A resolution slider in the UI is just switching layers of an already-computed tree — no recomputation. That's 80% of what people mean by "recluster in real time."
Filtering then reclustering (e.g., "only powershell-involving sessions, now recluster") is HDBSCAN over a subset of precomputed embeddings — at 500k points with cuML on GPU, that's seconds, fast enough for interactive use. Cache labels per (filter, layer) pair since analysts repeat filters.
For the front end, look hard at datamapplot — it's from the Tutte Institute, the same group as Toponymy, and it exists precisely to render these labeled hierarchical cluster maps as interactive HTML. Toponymy's layered labels slot into it natively. Don't build a bespoke viz layer before checking whether the companion tool already is one.
Overlay the gold labels as a toggle on the map (bad-user sessions as red points). This makes your harness metrics visible — when contamination is high you'll literally see red dust scattered through gray blobs, and when an embedding version fixes it you'll see red constellations form. That's also the artifact that wins the team over in a way no table of numbers does.

The dependency picture
Trajectory corpus (step 1) → v1 n-gram embedding (step 2) → harness scores → v2 sequence encoder (step 3) → harness scores → map + interactivity (step 4, can start in parallel after step 2). The analyst conversation you want to have slots in anywhere, and its output edits the harness, not the embeddings.

Total: roughly three weeks to a live, labeled, multi-resolution map backed by a sequence-trained embedding, with two scored decision points along the way.
